If you are a shipper moving freight out of the Baja California region this week, you are likely watching the news with a growing sense of frustration.

A major union strike has triggered rolling blockades at critical entry points into Tijuana, including the San Ysidro Port of Entry, the Tijuana-Tecate highway, and surrounding toll booths. **While we are highly sympathetic to the workers advocating for their livelihoods and understand the real, human concerns at the heart of this pension dispute, the resulting impact on cross-border trade is severe.**

With these massive U.S.-Mexico gateways virtually paralyzed, thousands of truckloads are currently stranded. Factory lines are bracing for delays, and transportation budgets are taking a massive hit. When a localized dispute causes a massive disruption at the border, it exposes the hidden vulnerabilities in your supply chain. Here is what the current Tijuana blockades are teaching the industry, and why the smartest manufacturers are building their resilience through the Arizona-Sonora corridor.

### The Danger of the "Mega-Crossing" Bottleneck

For years, the default strategy for West Coast cross-border freight has been to funnel everything through massive hubs like Tijuana and San Ysidro. But when you rely heavily on the most congested, politically sensitive border crossings, your entire supply chain can be held hostage by a local strike.

When a blockade happens at one of these mega-crossings, the backlog doesn't just clear up overnight. The ripple effects of a multi-day closure can cause weeks of capacity shortages and missed delivery windows.

### The "Broker-Only" Failure

Times of crisis also expose the fatal flaw of the "broker-only" logistics model. If your provider does not actually own the trucks moving your freight, they have zero agility when a blockade hits.

A digital broker with a slick app cannot force a third-party driver to reroute from a blocked toll booth in Tecate, nor can they guarantee your load won't be dropped for a higher-paying spot rate. When the border freezes, broker-only platforms can do nothing but send you an automated "delayed" email while you scramble for answers.

The route from Hermosillo directly up Highway 15 to the Mariposa Port of Entry in Nogales offers a highly secure, high-speed alternative to the traditional border bottlenecks. Because it is geographically insulated from the union unrest currently impacting Tijuana, the Nogales gateway remains wide open, fully operational, and specifically tailored for high-value freight.
